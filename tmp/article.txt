GENETICS AND DNA: CRISPR And Gene Editing 
Gene editing changes DNA to fix problems or add features, like editing a sentence in a book. CRISPR acts as tiny molecular scissors that cut DNA at exact spots and paste in new code very carefully.



How CRISPR Works

CRISPR uses guide RNA to find a DNA target, then Cas9 protein cuts it precisely. Cells repair the break by deleting faulty parts or adding new ones. It's cheap, fast, and more accurate than old methods.



Uses





Medicine: Fixes sickle cell disease by correcting blood genes; treats blindness in kids.



Agriculture: Makes pest-resistant corn or drought-proof rice without chemicals.



Animals: Edits pigs to resist diseases, improving meat safety.



Environment: Creates mosquitoes that can't spread malaria.



Concerns





Mistakes: Off-target cuts might change wrong DNA spots, causing health risks.



Ethics: Editing embryos could lead to "designer babies" for rich families only.



Ecosystems: Edited crops or animals might spread and harm wild species.



Myths vs Facts

1. Myth: CRISPR makes superhumans today.

    Fact: It only fixes single diseases—no super strength or IQ boosts yet.

2. Myth: Every edit passes to your kids.

    Fact: Body cell edits don't; only embryo edits might.

3.Myth: It's 100% safe and perfect.

   Fact: About 99% accurate but needs more long-term safety tests.

4.Myth: Only used for scary stuff.

    Fact: Mostly helps health and food—strict rules control risky uses.
